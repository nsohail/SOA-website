About Me

Major Art and Visual Technology (Graphic Design)

Degree BFA Art and Visual Technology

Creating an experience and environment for the viewer to submerse themselves in is what makes for a successful design. Growing up most of my life overseas, I feel that I bring unique world experiences to the table and a keen understanding of how to communicate effectively with a variety of different people. After taking many years off to experience the professional world, I decided to finish my education at George Mason University with a Bachelor of Fine Arts in Graphic Design. Working for clients with different design needs has given me distinctive experience in a variety of design fields. My style tends to lean towards minimalism, letting the product or business speak for itself. Copywriting, product development, and branding are a few areas that I really enjoy working on. Always ready for a challenge, I listen intently to client wishes, meet strict deadlines, and pay close attention to detail. When I’m not letting my creative juices flow, I can be found enjoying my free time reading a Michael Crichton novel, playing Super Mario Bros., or channeling my inner Ann Miller during a tap dance class. I call Virginia home alongside my husband, Brian, and my two cats. My senior project has given me the opportunity to create and design a book that has personal significance to me. I chose to write about pixel art, because it has influenced me to become an artist and designer. After playing video and computer games hour after hour, I was enamored with the narrative and artistic capabilities that games could create. Immersing myself into the game world was an escape, being able to interact with intriguing characters, imbibe in the endorphins of winning a level, or play God over a city that I created. I aim to show my audience that pixel illustrations are a timeless digital medium that will continue to be appealing far into the future. A few chuckles while reading about some of my own experiences wouldn’t hurt either. Some might say, “What can multiple little boxes arranged on a screen really create?” I would say the only limitations that exist are the ones within your imagination.

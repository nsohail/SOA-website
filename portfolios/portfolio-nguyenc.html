About Me

Major Art and Visual Technology (Graphic Design)

Degree BFA Art and Visual Technology

It all began with Clifford the Big Red Dog. That was my first reading experience. In the decades that followed, things haven’t changed much. The books I read nowadays are longer and more advanced, but I still experience a book in the same way I did as a kindergartener. Books present different ways of seeing the world, which spurs and stretches my imagination to accommodate these views. This is especially true when it comes to the subject of my senior project: science fiction. The authors of sci-fi stories create worlds and characters that are often completely alien to readers. The stories selected for my book contain time-traveling AIs, gargantuan sandworms, and a world where the calorie is the dominate source of currency. And even though these books seem farfetched, every one of them contains a kernel of truth originating from the real world. Reading sci-fi is a way to entertain spectacular ideas that can widen our perceptions of what is and isn’t possible. But before anything, the book has got to be a good yarn. The best sci-fi contains evocative storytelling that’s compelling to read. This often comes in the form of a fictional universe that is both grand in scope and nuanced in detail. Skilled authors don’t just say that intergalactic space travel exists, they show it by delving into things like the infrastructure, technology, and governance required to realize such a feat. Graphic designers face similar challenges. A great idea, even if grand and heartfelt, will still fall short if not supported by good craft. It’s not enough for me to just say that sci-fi is one of the most influential and unique genres in literature. I must demonstrate and communicate this opinion in a visually convincing manner (and ostensibly show what I’ve learned in the GMU design program). To that end, I have set out to create a book that explores sci-fi literature written across seven decades while also sharing my appreciation for the genre. Works like Neuromancer, Hyperion, and Ancillary Justice are more than just stories, they are worlds unto themselves.

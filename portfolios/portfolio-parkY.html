About Me

Major Art and Visual Technology (Drawing)

Design Portfolio

“Comfort women” were women and girls forced into sexual slavery by the Imperial Japanese Army before and during World War II. The name “comfort women” is a translation of the Japanese euphemism ianfu (慰安婦) and the similar Korean term wianbu (위안부). < Comfort Women—Wikipedia >

My work talks about the tragic history of “comfort women.” It has been already more than two years since I have started working on this issue. Last year, I had an opportunity to meet “comfort women” survivors in person in New York and Seoul, South Korea. I was very impressed that they did not just remain only as victims, but acted for the future generations and spoke so that the same tragedies would stop repeating.

My work invites viewers to experience and to communicate “comfort women’s” feelings and sadness, but also the hope. I intended to bring the viewers into the work and feel their own raw feelings to the tragic history. I reproduced two of the original paintings by “comfort women” to step closer to historical facts. I combined themes from contemporary media with traditional images. I tried to create visceral images by employing perspectives, lights, raw materials and textures.

The titles are to guide the viewers understand the emotional elements in the work. The thirteen to nineteen year old flower buds were trampled even before they could bloom. For more than a half century they had to remain silent, hidden in the shadow of history.

It is now the time to hear their lamentation.
